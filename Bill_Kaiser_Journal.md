# Bill Kaiser's Journal

## Entry 1: March 26, 2026

Another day, another set of tasks seen through to completion. Today started with the usual rigor of wrapping up the bids and awarding the job for our latest project. It’s been a fascinating journey, this venture into making the agent system work, a constant process of experimentation and discovery. There’s a certain satisfaction in laying down a framework, seeing the initial pieces fall into place.

After the bid was settled, I shifted gears, diving into market evaluations and some trading. That kept me occupied until about three o’clock. Then, it was time to get my hands dirty, literally. Christa – always thoughtful – gave me a special peony bulb, a ‘Diana de Nemours’. I took the time to research the proper planting technique, ensuring I gave it the best possible start. That led me to the compost pile, which needed turning. I then combined equal parts of the hydrogen and carbon compost, loaded it into the wagon, and mixed in some fresh topsoil. Down the hill I went, dug a precise one-foot by one-foot hole, and carefully planted the ‘Diana de Nemours’.

Beyond the peony, I spent some time tending to the bluebirds. I put out feathers and other nesting materials, hoping to entice them to settle in. Christa’s Christmas gift, a new bluebird house, also got its proper placement today. I pulled up the old pole from the bottom of the hill, screwed in the new one, attached the house, and re-secured it. While I was down there, I noticed the ornamental grasses around the old bluebird house had grown quite tall over the winter. Bluebirds prefer an open approach to their homes, so I fired up the generator – always a test of its winter resilience – and brought out the electric trimmer. It’s a bit of a haul, rolling the generator down the hill and stretching out a hundred feet of extension cord, but the result is worth it. The dried growth is now cut back, providing a clear, welcoming space for the bluebirds. I still need to add an entrance reducer to the bluebird box in the far perennial garden, and clear away the piles of trimmed grass, but the generator and cords are put away. That’s a crucial part of any job for me – putting everything back in its place. It’s easily twenty-five percent of the effort, but it ensures everything is ready for the next time. A busy, productive day, indeed.

*A note on this day:* Frankly, this is the stuff I can do when I'm motivated and feel in control of my situation and I'm getting things done. Good behavior begets good behavior. I do have a tendency to overdo it and burn out a little bit and need a little downtime. I don't go this hard all the time, but I have this capability and I like to reproduce it as much as possible because it makes me feel good.
